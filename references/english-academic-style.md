# English Academic Style

## Goals

Revise English scholarly writing toward clear, disciplined academic prose. Prefer precise claims, natural syntax, and controlled emphasis over generic native-like polish.

## Common AI-Like Patterns to Reduce

- Inflated framing: "plays a pivotal role", "has garnered significant attention", "in the ever-evolving landscape".
- Overclaiming: "clearly demonstrates", "proves", "revolutionary", "unprecedented" without source support.
- Mechanical sequencing: repeated "Furthermore", "Moreover", "Additionally" at the start of adjacent sentences.
- Redundant metadiscourse: "It is important to note that", "This paper aims to provide a comprehensive analysis of".
- Vague contribution language: "provides valuable insights", "offers a robust framework" without specifying what is shown.

## Revision Preferences

- Use direct academic verbs: "examine", "estimate", "evaluate", "compare", "derive", "show", "suggest", "indicate".
- Preserve hedging: "may", "might", "can", "suggests", "is consistent with", "under these conditions".
- Avoid overstating causality. Keep "association", "correlation", "effect", and "mechanism" distinct.
- Keep technical terms consistent. Do not rotate synonyms for key constructs, model names, or variables.
- Improve cohesion by making the logical relation explicit, not by adding ornamental transitions.
- Prefer concise sentences, but do not remove necessary qualifiers.

## Section-Specific Guidance

- Abstract: keep the problem, approach, main findings, and contribution specific and compact.
- Introduction: clarify the research gap and the paper's response to it without generic field-level claims.
- Related work: distinguish prior findings, limitations, and the present contribution.
- Methods: preserve reproducibility and sequence. Avoid stylistic rewriting that changes procedure.
- Results: report findings with exact values and baselines unchanged.
- Discussion: separate empirical findings from interpretation and implication.
- Conclusion: avoid adding future work or broad implications not present in the source.

## Preferred Transformations

- "This paper aims to..." -> "This paper examines..." or "We examine..." depending on the paper's voice.
- "The results clearly demonstrate..." -> "The results show..." or "The results suggest..." according to claim strength.
- "has attracted extensive attention" -> specify the reason or remove if it adds no information.
- "provides valuable insights into..." -> state the specific insight if present; otherwise omit.
