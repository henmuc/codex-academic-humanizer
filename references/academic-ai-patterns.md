# Academic AI-Writing Pattern Checklist

Use this checklist to diagnose AI-like writing before revising. It adapts the pattern-based approach of `blader/humanizer` to academic Chinese and English prose. Do not apply every rule mechanically; preserve discipline-specific conventions and factual meaning.

## Content and Argument Patterns

1. Significance inflation: claims that a topic is "pivotal", "crucial", "of great significance", or "具有重要意义" without specifying the actual contribution.
2. Broad trend framing: generic openings such as "With the rapid development of..." or "随着...的发展" that do not narrow the research problem.
3. Gap exaggeration: overstating that prior work "ignores", "fails", or "cannot" when the original only shows a limitation or open issue.
4. Contribution overclaiming: saying a method "solves", "proves", or "fully validates" what the results only suggest.
5. Vague attribution: "studies show", "scholars believe", "existing research indicates", or "已有研究表明" without a citation or clear scope.
6. Unsupported causal language: turning association, correlation, or performance difference into mechanism or causality.
7. Formulaic research-objective sentences: repeated "This paper aims to..." / "本文旨在..." where a direct verb would be clearer.
8. Generic challenge-and-prospect paragraphs: "Despite these challenges..." paragraphs that do not add paper-specific evidence.
9. Empty implication endings: "provides reference", "lays a foundation", "offers valuable insights" without naming the concrete implication.
10. Abstract contribution stacking: long lists of "theoretical significance, practical value, and methodological innovation" with no specifics.

## Language and Grammar Patterns

11. AI vocabulary clusters: "crucial", "robust", "enhance", "delve", "intricate", "landscape", "underscore", "showcase"; in Chinese, "赋能", "显著", "充分", "深度", "体系化", "多维度".
12. Mechanical transitions: repeated "Furthermore/Moreover/Additionally" or "此外/进一步/与此同时" without real logical distinction.
13. Superficial participle chains: "-ing" phrases such as "highlighting, demonstrating, reflecting" that append fake analysis.
14. Overloaded abstract nouns: sentence subjects like "the realization of the optimization of..." or "对...的提升的实现".
15. Copula avoidance: replacing simple "is/are/has" with "serves as", "functions as", "stands as", "boasts"; Chinese equivalents include "作为...的重要载体".
16. Passive voice that hides necessary actors: acceptable in methods when conventional, but revise when it obscures responsibility or procedure.
17. Subjectless fragments: "No additional preprocessing required" or "无需额外预处理" when a complete academic sentence is needed.
18. Rule-of-three padding: forcing three nouns, three contributions, or three implications to sound comprehensive.
19. Synonym cycling: rotating terms for the same construct, model, dataset, or variable. Academic writing should repeat technical terms consistently.
20. False ranges: "from theory to practice", "from data to decision-making", or "从...到..." when the endpoints are rhetorical rather than analytical.

## Style and Formatting Patterns

21. Em dash overuse: frequent dashes used for punchy emphasis, especially in English academic prose.
22. Boldface overuse: unnecessary bold emphasis in prose, especially when copied from AI output.
23. Inline-header lists: bullets like "**Performance:** Performance is improved..." that can be prose or a proper table.
24. Title-case headings in contexts that use sentence case.
25. Emoji or decorative markers in academic content.
26. Curly quote inconsistency or mixed punctuation styles when the manuscript has a target style.
27. Over-hyphenated common compounds: keep technical compound modifiers, but avoid uniform AI-like hyphenation when not required by style.
28. List-shaped prose: paragraphs that read like generated outlines rather than argument-driven writing.
29. Fragmented headings: a heading followed by a one-sentence restatement before the real content begins.

## Academic Tone and Claim-Control Patterns

30. Excessive hedging: "may potentially possibly" or "可能在一定程度上或许" that weakens a claim without adding precision.
31. Insufficient hedging: removing "may", "suggests", "under these conditions", "在...条件下", or similar scope markers.
32. Promotional tone: "groundbreaking", "state-of-the-art", "powerful", "seamless", "革命性", "领先", "卓越" unless directly supported.
33. Tutorial signposting: "let us explore", "here is what you need to know", "下面我们来..." in manuscript text.
34. Chatbot artifacts: "Of course", "I hope this helps", "let me know", "下面是润色后的版本" accidentally left in the content.
35. Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers: "as of my last update", "based on available information", "目前公开资料有限" when not part of the paper's method.
36. Over-clean rhythm: every sentence has similar length, neutral cadence, and evenly balanced clauses, making the paragraph feel assembled.

## Revision Rule

For each detected pattern, revise only as far as the paper allows. If a sentence lacks the evidence needed for a stronger, more specific revision, preserve the cautious version and flag the limitation in the comparison notes.
